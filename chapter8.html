Chapter 8: When Nobody Was Watching

June. Aavni’s house.

It was a late-June afternoon when the silence outside mirrored the storm brewing inside her chest. Aavni’s parents were out of town. The house stood quiet. So did her nerves.

She glanced at her phone.
1 New Message — Darshan:
“I’m in front of your house. Come fast or I’ll shout ‘Aavni, I love you!’ from here.”

She rushed to the gate, hands slightly trembling, barefoot, in an oversized tee and soft cotton shorts — nothing too obvious, yet not innocent either. Her heart thudded as she twisted the key and opened the gate.

There he stood — leaning against his Activa, hair ruffled, wearing that grey tee she secretly loved. His eyes scanned her from head to toe and back, settling on her flustered smile.

“You sure?” he asked, half teasing, half nervous.

“Too late to ask,” she smirked, locking the gate behind him.

Inside the house, the world fell away. No horns, no colony aunties, no calls. Just silence, four walls… and them.

He sat on the sofa, pretending to be calm. She handed him water, her fingers brushing his. Electricity. She turned to walk away, but he held her wrist.

“Wait,” he said softly. “Why are you nervous now?”

“Because you’re here. And I know what that means,” she whispered.

A pause. Then he stood. Inches away. One step forward, and their noses almost touched.

“What does it mean?”
“It means I won’t be able to stop myself…”

Touches

He leaned in, cupped her face gently, and kissed her — soft at first. Just lips brushing, a test, a tease. Then again. Deeper. More urgent. His hands moved to her waist, her fingers sliding into his hair.

“Wait,” she whispered. “I don’t want to rush…”
“We’re not,” he replied, forehead against hers. “We’re home.”

He lifted her effortlessly, her legs wrapping around his waist instinctively. Their laughter slipped into breathless kisses as he carried her to the bedroom.

He laid her gently on the bed like she was made of moonlight.
Kissed her forehead. Then her cheek. Then her neck. Down to her collarbone.

“You smell like my favourite dream,” he murmured.

Her hands tugged his tee off. His lips found her waist, her stomach, the soft curve of her hip.

She gasped — not out of shock, but out of finally, finally feeling him that close.

“You're trembling,” he whispered.
“I'm not scared,” she said. “I'm yours.”

They kissed like they were drinking each other in. Like the world might vanish if they stopped. Every layer of cloth removed was like another wall falling between them.

His fingers explored slowly — not greedy, but reverent. Her hands traced his back, his chest, memorizing. The room smelled of skin and whispered names and sighs that only two hearts could understand.

Later, tangled in a messy bedsheet, her face resting on his chest, he played with her hair. They said nothing for minutes. Just soft giggles, breath syncing, hearts still racing.

“You’re dangerous,” he whispered.
“You kissed me first,” she shot back.
He laughed. “Tani would be proud.”

“She literally taught me how to kiss on hand and said: ‘Upper lip pe pehle jaana, bhul mat.’”

“And did you?”
“You tell me,” she grinned.

He kissed her again, slow and deep.

They changed into comfy clothes. Aavni walked into the kitchen, still barefoot, and made chai. He watched her from the door, head tilted.

“You know,” he said, “your lips are sweeter than this tea.”
She rolled her eyes, cheeks blushing. “Bas kar Darshu…”

The Panic & Goodbye

As the clock hit 6:45, panic crept back. She peeked out the curtain. No one in sight. Still…

“You have to go,” she said, chest tight.
“I know. But I’ll be back.”

He kissed her forehead. “Thank you for letting me in…”
“I don’t just mean the house,” he added.

She hugged him tight before unlocking the gate.

He vanished into the evening as quickly as he came, and Aavni stood there, barefoot, tea in hand, smiling to herself like a girl who had just lived her favorite chapter.

“It wasn’t about crossing lines.
It was about knowing…
I trusted him enough to open the door.”
